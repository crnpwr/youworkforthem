Landlords of Parliament

The UK is in the midst of a housing crisis.
Rents are going up, which is damaging the UK economy and making life harder for millions of people.

Scroll down and we'll explore 171 rented properties in detail.

These are properties let out by MPs.

Most are residential, but some are agricultural or commercial.

There are 76 landlords in Parliament, who you can see here.

Many constituents will struggle when rents go up, while that means more profit to these MPs.

Some of them have pretty big property empires.

Let's take a look at the biggest landlord in Parliament, Jas Athwal.
He's the Labour MP for Ilford South.

He's collecting rent for eighteen properties, bringing in at least £180,000 per year.
Fifteen of these are residential and three are commercial.

His residential properties have received criticism for their poor condition.

His commercial properties have also had issues.

One is a children's home that is "poorly maintained".
Ofsted has been worried about how many children go missing from the home.

Let's take another example, Rachel Reeves - Chancellor of the Exchequer.

She's not as prolific a landlord as others, and she only has one residential let listed.

The register of interests only lets us know that she makes at least £10,000 per year this way.

But the Telegraph says that her and her husband actually make £74,000 per year from renters.

She has a salary of £163,891 and her husband's was over £170,000 in 2024, so between them that's an income of at least £407,891.

This is 11 times the typical family income of £36,700.

This might make it hard for her to relate to the struggles of ordinary people on issues like benefit cuts.

Until recently, Rushanara Ali was homelessness minister for England.

She has two residential lets listed, and we found out from a former tenant that she was making £39,600 from one of them.

She evicted those tenants, saying she was going to sell the house, but then she let it out again for £48,000 per year.

She was forced to resign as homelessness minister over this, losing £22,375 of her salary.

Sadly for her, this canceled out the extra money she was able to get from the new tenants.

23 landlords voted against the Renters' Rights Bill, which was designed to protect renters from unfair evictions.

They tried to maintain their rights as landlords, as opposed to many of the renters they represent.

This is just a peek at the landlords of Parliament, based on data collected on 21st August, 2025.
