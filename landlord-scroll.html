The housing crisis and skyrocketing rents are wreaking havoc on the economy of the UK and the wellbeing of its citizens.

Scroll down and we'll explore 171 rented properties in detail.

These are the residential, commercial and agricultural properties let out by MPs. As we'll show, the data isn't complete but we are able to get a rough picture.

There are 76 landlords in Parliament, who you can see here.

They only have to list the properties they get more than £10,000 per year for, so there are probably more out there.

Some of them have pretty big property empires.

Let's take a look at the biggest landlord in Parliament, Jas Athwal - the Labour MP for Ilford South.

He's collecting rent for 15 residential properties and 3 commercial properties, bringing in over £180,000 per year.

His residential properties have received criticism for their poor condition.

He's also been criticised for his commercial lettings.

Let's take another example, Rachel Reeves - Chancellor of the Exchequer.

She's not as prolific a landlord as others, and she only has one residential let listed.

The register of interests only lets us know that she makes £10,000 per year from this.

Reporting from the Telegraph suggests that her and her husband actually bring in £74,000 per year for their two flats.

Taking in to account her £163,891 salary and her husband's reported salary of over £170,000 in 2024, we can see their total household income is at least £407,891.

This is eleven times the median household income of £36,700, which may make it hard for her to relate to the struggles of ordinary people on issues like benefit cuts.

Similarly, we could look at Rushanara Ali - who until recently was homelessness minister for England.

She has two residential lets listed, but we found out from a former tenant that she was making £39,600 from one of them.

She went as far as evicting existing tenants so she could raise this to £48,000 per year - having told them she had plans to sell the house.

She was forced to resign as homelessness minister over this.

Sadly for her, this meant losing £22,375 in ministerial salary, more than cancelling out the extra money she was able to extract from the new tenants.

If this all feels a little academic, it might be worth noting that many of these MPs have actively opposed renters' rights in Parliament by voting against the Renters' Rights Bill.

As landlords, they resisted moves that could protect renters from unfair evictions and looked to strengthen their own rights, in many cases against the people they're meant to represent.

This has been a guided walkthrough of the role of landlords in Parliament and their relation to the housing crisis, based on data collected on 21st August, 2025.
